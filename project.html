Aim

To study and solve Heat Flow problems using Linear Differential Equations (LDE). The experiment demonstrates the application of Newton’s Law of Cooling for transient systems and Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction for steady-state temperature distribution equations in mechanical engineering systems.

Theory

Heat flow is the transfer of thermal energy from a higher temperature body to a lower temperature body. The fundamental principles involved in the problems of heat conduction are:

  • Heat flows from a higher temperature to the lower temperature.
  • The quantity of heat in a body is proportional to its mass and temperature.

1. Steady-State Conduction: Fourier's Law

The rate of heat flow across an area is proportional to the area and to the rate of change of temperature with respect to its distance normal to the area. If $q$ is the quantity of heat flowing across a slab of area $A$, the mathematical representation is:

$$q = -kA\frac{dT}{dx}$$

Where $k$ is a constant depending upon the material of the body called the thermal conductivity. The negative sign is attached because temperature $T$ decreases as distance $x$ increases.

Application: Cylindrical Pipe Insulation

For a steady-state heat loss per unit time from a pipe of inner radius $r_0$ at temperature $T_0$, covered with insulation of outer radius $r_1$ at temperature $T_1$, the heat flow $Q$ is given by integrating Fourier's equation:

$$Q = \frac{2\pi k(T_0 - T_1)}{\ln(r_1) - \ln(r_0)}$$

2. Transient Heat Flow: Newton's Law of Cooling

Newton’s Law of Cooling states that the rate of cooling of a body is directly proportional to the difference between the body temperature and the surrounding ambient temperature. The temperature variation with time can be represented using Linear Differential Equations.

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a)$$

Integrating this yields the final temperature formula:

$$T = T_a + (T_0 - T_a)e^{-kt}$$
  • $T$ = final temperature after time $t$
  • $T_0$ = initial temperature
  • $T_a$ = surrounding (ambient) temperature
  • $k$ = cooling constant

Engineering Applications

  • Industrial cooling systems & automotive heat exchangers.
  • Thermal engineering and heat conduction analysis.
  • Environmental heat studies and steady-state pipe insulation optimization.
